# Complete Project Context

## 1. Why recursive training matters

Synthetic text is becoming common online. If later language models train on data generated by earlier language models, training becomes recursive: model generation `g` helps create the data used to train generation `g+1`.

Repeated training on imperfect generated distributions can reinforce errors, reduce diversity, and lose rare parts of the original human distribution. This repository uses **recursive-training degradation** as the cautious general term. “Model collapse” is used only with the scope supported by cited literature.

## 2. The resource-allocation question

Human-origin data may be valuable but finite, licensed, costly to curate, or strategically reserved. Rather than asking only whether human data helps, this project treats it as a lifetime resource-allocation problem.

Let:

- `G` be the recursive horizon;
- `M` be the number of monitored human-distribution modes;
- `B` be the lifetime number of human-origin optimizer-consumed tokens;
- `b[g,m]` be the human-token allocation to generation `g` and mode `m`.

Every budget-matched policy must satisfy:

```text
sum over generations g and modes m of b[g,m] = B
```

The comparison also fixes total optimizer-consumed tokens so a method cannot win merely by training longer.

## 3. What counts as a human token

The budget counts token presentations actually consumed by the optimizer, not unique documents or approximate character counts. If a 100-token human example is presented in three epochs, it consumes 300 human-origin optimizer tokens. Padding is excluded or handled through one frozen documented rule.

## 4. Treatment families

### Fresh random rescue

Use human data selected randomly under a frozen allocation schedule.

### Schedule-only allocation

Choose how much budget to spend at each generation, but do not target particular modes beyond a frozen neutral sampling rule.

### Selection-only allocation

Target under-covered modes, but follow a fixed time schedule.

### Joint time-and-mode allocation

Use the monitored chain state to choose both when to spend and which modes receive the human-data budget.

### No rescue

A reference chain with no added human rescue. It is scientifically helpful but is not the central comparison.

## 5. Main hypothesis

At the same lifetime human-origin optimizer-token budget and total optimizer-token budget, the joint policy will reduce chain-level degradation relative to the strongest schedule-only and selection-only baselines.

The hypothesis is unsupported until the frozen pilot completes. A favorable toy simulation is an implementation check, not scientific evidence.

## 6. Monitoring bias

A targeted policy observes a monitoring reference, not the full human distribution. If the reference omits an important mode, the policy may spend its budget efficiently according to the wrong target. The project therefore treats monitoring bias as both a robustness test and a possible failure-boundary contribution.

## 7. Primary outcomes

### Held-out human negative log-likelihood

Held-out human NLL measures fit to human test text that is never used for prompting, selection, tuning, or early stopping.

### NLL-regret area under the recursive curve

For each complete chain, generation-wise regret is aggregated across the frozen horizon. This avoids presenting only the most favorable generation.

### Tail retention

Tail retention measures preservation of frozen underrepresented human modes. Its definition must be reliable and must not simply duplicate the policy's own selection score.

## 8. Experimental unit

One independently seeded complete recursive chain is one experimental unit. Ten generations inside one chain are dependent repeated observations, not ten independent samples.

## 9. Data partitions

The following must be disjoint by stable ID, content hash, and documented near-duplicate rules:

1. base human training data;
2. rescue candidates;
3. generation prompts;
4. validation data;
5. final human test data.

The final test partition cannot influence any policy or design decision.

- a universal collapse-prevention claim;
- proof of optimality;
- main-conference readiness.

## 11. Falsification and stop rules

The main hypothesis is not supported if the joint policy fails to exceed the strongest eligible non-joint baseline by the frozen meaningful effect, uncertainty includes a practically harmful/equivalent region, token budgets are mismatched, leakage occurs, or the outcome depends on post-hoc exclusions.

The project stops or narrows if novelty is already owned, the positive control cannot be reproduced, the pipeline cannot be validated, or a powered interpretation is unaffordable.
